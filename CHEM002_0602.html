Properties of Alcohols

In this lesson, we explore how the hydroxyl (–OH) group affects the physical and chemical properties of alcohols. These properties explain why alcohols behave very differently from hydrocarbons of similar size.

1. Polarity of Alcohols

Alcohols are polar molecules because of the highly electronegative oxygen atom in the –OH group. Oxygen pulls electron density toward itself, creating partial charges.

  • Oxygen: partial negative (δ–)
  • Hydrogen (in –OH): partial positive (δ+)

This polarity allows alcohol molecules to attract each other and interact strongly with polar substances like water.

Activity 1: Polarity Check

Which part of an alcohol molecule is responsible for its polarity?

The hydroxyl (–OH) group is responsible because oxygen is much more electronegative than hydrogen and carbon.

2. Hydrogen Bonding

Alcohols can form hydrogen bonds — strong intermolecular attractions between:

  • The hydrogen of one –OH group
  • The oxygen of another –OH group

Hydrogen bonding is much stronger than ordinary dipole–dipole forces and greatly affects boiling point and solubility.

Hydrogen bonds form between –OH groups of neighboring alcohol molecules.

Activity 2: Hydrogen Bonding

Why can alcohols form hydrogen bonds but alkanes cannot?

Alcohols contain O–H bonds where hydrogen is bonded to a highly electronegative oxygen. Alkanes only contain C–H bonds, which are not polar enough for hydrogen bonding.

3. Boiling Points of Alcohols

Alcohols have much higher boiling points than alkanes of similar molecular mass. This is due to hydrogen bonding between alcohol molecules.

  • Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): boiling point ≈ 78°C
  • Ethane (C₂H₆): boiling point ≈ –89°C

As the carbon chain length increases, boiling point increases due to stronger dispersion forces.

Activity 3: Compare Boiling Points

Which has the higher boiling point: propanol or propane? Explain why.

Propanol has the higher boiling point because it can form hydrogen bonds, while propane cannot.

4. Solubility of Alcohols in Water

Lower alcohols (methanol, ethanol, propanol) are miscible with water because their –OH groups form hydrogen bonds with water molecules.

As the hydrocarbon chain becomes longer:

  • The nonpolar part dominates
  • Solubility in water decreases

Activity 4: Solubility Trend

Which is more soluble in water: ethanol or pentanol?

Ethanol is more soluble because it has a shorter nonpolar hydrocarbon chain.

5. Acidity of Alcohols

Alcohols are weak acids. They can donate a proton (H⁺) from the –OH group, but only to a very small extent.

Compared to water:

  • Alcohols are slightly less acidic
  • Alkyl groups push electron density toward oxygen, making proton loss harder

Activity 5: Acid Strength

Are alcohols strong acids, weak acids, or neutral?

Alcohols are weak acids. They only partially donate protons in solution.

6. Practice Problems

1️⃣ Which property of alcohols mainly explains their high boiling points?

Hydrogen bonding between –OH groups.

2️⃣ Why does solubility decrease as alcohol chain length increases?

The nonpolar hydrocarbon chain becomes dominant, reducing interaction with water.

3️⃣ Which interacts more strongly with water: an alkane or an alcohol?

An alcohol, due to polarity and hydrogen bonding.

Key Terms

  • Hydroxyl group (–OH): Functional group that defines alcohols.
  • Polarity: Unequal distribution of electrons in a bond.
  • Hydrogen bonding: Strong intermolecular force involving O–H bonds.
  • Boiling point: Temperature at which a liquid turns to gas.
  • Miscible: Completely soluble in another liquid.
  • Weak acid: Substance that only partially donates protons.
