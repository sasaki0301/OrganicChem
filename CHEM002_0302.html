Formal Charges

Formal charge is a bookkeeping tool chemists use to assign charges to atoms in Lewis structures. It helps decide the best Lewis structure (resonance contributors) and predict reactivity and stability.

1. What is Formal Charge?

The formal charge (FC) on an atom is the hypothetical charge the atom would have if electrons in all bonds were shared equally between the bonded atoms. It is not the same as the actual partial charge (which comes from electronegativity differences), but it helps compare Lewis structures.

Formula (simple and reliable)

Formal charge = Valence electrons (free atom) − Nonbonding electrons − ½ × Bonding electrons

Or equivalently: FC = V − (N + B/2), where V = valence electrons, N = nonbonding electrons, B = bonding electrons.

Quick Example: Formal charge on oxygen in H2O (Lewis: H–O–H)

Oxygen valence electrons V = 6. Nonbonding electrons N = 4 (two lone pairs). Bonding electrons B = 4 (two O–H bonds; 2 electrons each).

Calculation: FC = 6 − (4 + 4/2) = 6 − (4 + 2) = 0. So oxygen has a formal charge of 0 in the neutral water molecule.

2. Why Formal Charges Matter

  • They help decide which Lewis structure (or resonance form) is most important — most stable structures have small (near zero) formal charges and place negative formal charges on the more electronegative atoms.
  • They explain where charges reside in ions and molecules and help predict reactive sites (nucleophiles/electrophiles).

Resonance structures often differ by electron placement; formal charge helps weigh contributors.

3. Step-by-step: How to assign formal charges

  1. Draw a reasonable Lewis structure (count total valence electrons and place bonds & lone pairs).
  2. For each atom: find V (valence electrons in free atom).
  3. Count N = nonbonding electrons (lone-pair electrons belonging to that atom).
  4. Count B = total bonding electrons around that atom (each bond contributes 2 electrons).
  5. Compute FC = V − (N + B/2).
  6. Check that the sum of formal charges equals the overall molecular charge (0 for neutrals, ±n for ions).
  7. Prefer structures with smaller magnitudes of formal charge, and with negative FC on more electronegative atoms.

Practice: Determine formal charges in CO2 (structure O=C=O)

Use the step-by-step method for each atom.

For central C: V=4, N=0 (no lone pairs), B=8 (four bonds counted as electrons) → FC = 4 − (0 + 8/2) = 4 − 4 = 0.
For each O (double-bonded): V=6, N=4 (two lone pairs), B=4 (two bonds) → FC = 6 − (4 + 4/2) = 6 − (4 + 2) = 0. All atoms have FC = 0; CO2 is well represented by this structure.

4. Resonance and Formal Charges — Which is better?

Formal charges guide which resonance structures are more important. Example: nitrite ion (NO2−) has resonance forms with a negative formal charge on one O in each form; both contributors matter.

Example A: Nitrite, NO2− (two resonance forms)

Construct two resonance Lewis structures with one double-bonded O and one single-bonded O bearing the negative charge.

Structure 1: O(=)N–O(−) and Structure 2: O(−)–N(=)O. Formal charges: In the double-bond O: FC = 0; in the single-bond O: FC = −1; on N: FC = +1. The negative charge localizes on oxygen (more electronegative) — resonance spreads the negative charge over both oxygens, stabilizing the ion.

Example B: Ammonium, NH4+

What are the formal charges on N and H in NH4+?

Nitrogen: V=5, N=0 (no lone pairs), B=8 → FC = 5 − (0 + 8/2) = 5 − 4 = +1. Each H: V=1, N=0, B=2 → FC = 1 − (0 + 2/2) = 0. Sum = +1, matching the ion’s charge. Nitrogen bears the positive formal charge.

5. Practice Problems (scaffolded)

Problem 1: SO2 (draw structure and assign formal charges)

Try: one canonical structure has S with one double bond to each O; consider possible expanded octet for S.

One reasonable Lewis form: O=S=O with each O having two lone pairs; S has no lone pairs. For each O: V=6, N=4, B=4 → FC = 6 − (4 + 2) = 0. For S: V=6, N=0, B=8 → FC = 6 − (0 + 4) = +2. That gives +2 on S and 0 on O — but better representations include resonance and one structure with a S–O single bond and negative formal charge on O while S has a lower positive FC; sulfur can expand its octet (use carefully). Key idea: compare magnitudes and prefer structures that minimize large charges and place negative FC on more electronegative atoms.

Problem 2: Calculate formal charges in HCOO− (formate ion)

Draw resonance forms (one with double bond to one O and single to the other).

In each resonance form: one O is double-bonded (FC=0), the other O is single-bonded (FC=−1), C bears FC=+1, H is 0. Resonance distributes the negative charge between the two oxygens.

Problem 3 (challenge): In sulfate SO42−, what are the formal charges if every S–O is drawn as single bonds and you place the extra electrons on oxygen?

Consider alternative Lewis forms that include S=O double bonds and compare.

If all S–O are single bonds and every O has three lone pairs: each O FC = −1 (four oxygens × −1 = −4) and S FC = +6 (to balance total −2). That gives large charges. Better resonance forms include two S=O double bonds and two S–O single bonds (or equivalent resonance), which distribute charges: typical formal charges become S = +2 and some O = 0 and some O = −1 across resonance contributors. Real structure is a resonance hybrid and is stabilized by charge delocalization. Takeaway: minimized and delocalized formal charges produce more stable descriptions.

Key Terms & Definitions

TermDefinition
Formal chargeHypothetical charge assigned to an atom in a Lewis structure using FC = V − (N + B/2).
Valence electrons (V)Electrons in the outermost shell of a free atom used for bonding.
Nonbonding electrons (N)Electrons as lone pairs on an atom in the Lewis structure.
Bonding electrons (B)Total electrons shared in bonds around the atom (each bond counts as 2).
ResonanceDifferent valid Lewis structures for the same molecule; the actual structure is a hybrid of contributors.
Octet ruleThe tendency for atoms (mainly second-row elements) to have eight electrons in their valence shell.
ElectronegativityAbility of an atom to attract shared electrons; helps decide where negative formal charges are best placed.
DelocalizationSpreading of electrons across multiple atoms (resonance), which stabilizes the molecule/ion.
