Drawing Chemical Structures

This lesson will help you understand how chemists represent molecules — from Lewis structures to condensed and skeletal formulas — and how to visualize these in 3D.

1. Why Draw Chemical Structures?

Chemical structures help us visualize how atoms are connected in a molecule. These diagrams reveal bonding, lone pairs, geometry, and functional groups — essential in organic chemistry.

There are several ways to represent molecules:

  • Lewis structure: shows all valence electrons as dots.
  • Structural formula: shows bonds between atoms explicitly.
  • Condensed formula: groups atoms together without showing all bonds.
  • Skeletal (line-angle) formula: used in organic chemistry to simplify complex carbon chains.

Different representations of the same molecule (ethanol) — Lewis, structural, and skeletal forms.

2. Lewis Structures: The Basics

A Lewis structure shows valence electrons as dots. Shared pairs between atoms form covalent bonds (lines), and lone pairs remain as dots around the atom.

Boron is the central atom because it’s less electronegative than fluorine (and in any case, halogens such as F almost never form more than one single bond).
Boron has three valence electrons, fluorine has seven, and the charge on the molecule is –1, so the total number of valence electrons in this molecule is 3 + 7(4) + 1 = 32.
Adding single bonds from boron to each of the four fluorines (for a total of eight electrons, two per bond) and adding the remaining 24 electrons to the fluorines as lone pairs gives the Lewis structure shown.
Each atom has a full octet of electrons, so there’s no need to make multiple bonds.

Draw the Lewis Structure of NO2-

Nitrogen is the central atom in NO2- because nitrogen is less electronegative than oxygen.
Nitrogen has five valence electrons, oxygen has six, and the charge on the molecule is –1, so the molecule has 5 + 2(6) + 1 = 18 valence electrons.
Making single bonds from N to both oxygens (for a total of four electrons, two per bond) leaves 14 electrons.
Adding these electrons onto the oxygens until both oxygens have completed their octet still leaves two electrons left over.
Place these two electrons on the central nitrogen.
Examining this structure reveals that both oxygens have a complete octet, but nitrogen is still lacking two electrons.
So a lone pair on one of the oxygens is pushed onto the nitrogen to form a nitrogen-oxygen double bond.
Last, add the charge to complete the final structure.

3. Condensed and Skeletal Structures

For organic molecules, condensed and skeletal formulas make complex structures easier to read. Carbon atoms and C–H bonds are often not shown in skeletal formulas — every line end or intersection represents a carbon.

Skeletal structure of ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH).

Activity 2: Identify the Molecule

Look at the skeletal structure above. Write its condensed and molecular formula.

Condensed: CH₃CH₂OH
Molecular: C₂H₆O
This is ethanol — an alcohol with a hydroxyl (–OH) group.

Activity 3: Analyze Bond Geometry

In the ethanol molecule, identify which carbon is sp³-hybridized and explain why the O–H bond is polar.

Both carbons are sp³-hybridized (each forms four sigma bonds). The O–H bond is polar because oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen, pulling electron density toward itself.
