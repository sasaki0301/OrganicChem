Drawing Chemical Structures

In organic chemistry, drawings are not just illustrations—they communicate how atoms are connected, how electrons are arranged, and how molecules behave. This lesson introduces Lewis, structural, condensed, and skeletal formulas, along with 3D visualization.

1. Why Do We Draw Chemical Structures?

Chemists use different types of structures depending on what information they want to emphasize. Each representation highlights a different aspect of a molecule:

  • Lewis structure: shows valence electrons using dots and lines.
  • Structural formula: shows every covalent bond explicitly.
  • Condensed formula: groups atoms without showing all bonds.
  • Skeletal (line-angle) formula: the most efficient way to draw carbon chains.

2. Lewis Structures

Lewis structures show electrons as dots and bonds as lines. They help identify bonding pairs, lone pairs, and formal charges.

Activity 1: Lewis Structure of H2O

How many bonding pairs and lone pairs are around oxygen in a water molecule?

Hydrogen atoms each form one single bond with oxygen → 2 bonding pairs. Oxygen has 2 additional lone pairs → total = 2 bonding + 2 lone pairs.

Try this one: Draw BF4−.

Total electrons = 3 + 4×7 + 1 = 32. Boron forms four single bonds with fluorine; remaining electrons fill fluorine octets.

One more: Draw NO2− (nitrite ion).

Total electrons = 5 + 2×6 + 1 = 18. After placing bonds and octets, one oxygen forms a double bond; resonance is present.

3. Condensed and Skeletal Structures

In organic chemistry, long carbon chains are often drawn using skeletal structures. Each corner or endpoint is a carbon atom with enough hydrogens to complete its valence.

Activity 3: Analyze Molecular Geometry

Which atoms in ethanol are sp³-hybridized? Why is the O–H bond polar?

Both carbon atoms are sp³-hybridized because each forms four sigma bonds. The O–H bond is polar because oxygen is significantly more electronegative than hydrogen.

Key Terms

  • Lewis structure — electron-dot representation showing bonds and lone pairs.
  • Structural formula — shows all bonds explicitly.
  • Condensed formula — groups atoms without full bond display.
  • Skeletal formula — simplified carbon skeleton representation.
  • Valence electrons — outer electrons involved in bonding.
  • Molecular geometry — 3D arrangement of atoms.
