Chapter 6 Summary: Alcohols

In this chapter, we explored alcohols — their structure, naming, physical properties, and chemical behavior. This summary consolidates the key ideas you need before taking the chapter assessment.

1. What Are Alcohols?

Alcohols are organic compounds that contain a hydroxyl group (–OH) attached to a saturated carbon atom.

  • General formula: R–OH
  • Functional group: –OH (hydroxyl)
  • Alcohols are derived from alkanes by replacing a hydrogen with –OH

Checkpoint 1: Identify the Functional Group

Which part of an alcohol molecule is responsible for most of its chemical properties?

The hydroxyl group (–OH) controls polarity, hydrogen bonding, and acidity.

2. Naming Alcohols (IUPAC Overview)

Alcohols are named using standard IUPAC rules:

  • Choose the longest carbon chain containing the –OH group
  • Number the chain so the –OH gets the lowest possible number
  • Replace -ane with -ol
  • Indicate the position of the –OH if necessary

Checkpoint 2: Naming Logic

Why is the –OH group given priority in numbering?

The –OH group is the principal functional group and determines the suffix, so it must receive the lowest locant.

3. Physical Properties of Alcohols

Alcohols display unique physical properties due to the polarity of the O–H bond.

  • Alcohols are polar molecules
  • They form hydrogen bonds with each other and with water
  • They have higher boiling points than alkanes of similar size
  • Short-chain alcohols are miscible with water

Checkpoint 3: Boiling Point Trend

Why does ethanol boil at a much higher temperature than ethane?

Ethanol forms hydrogen bonds due to its –OH group, while ethane only has weak dispersion forces.

4. Solubility in Water

Alcohol solubility depends on the balance between the polar –OH group and the nonpolar hydrocarbon chain.

  • Short-chain alcohols: highly soluble
  • Long-chain alcohols: limited solubility
  • Increasing carbon length decreases solubility

Checkpoint 4: Predict Solubility

Which is more soluble in water: propanol or hexanol?

Propanol — it has a shorter hydrocarbon chain and stronger interaction with water.

5. Acidity of Alcohols

Alcohols are weak acids because they can donate a proton from the O–H bond.

  • Less acidic than water
  • Much weaker acids than carboxylic acids
  • Acidity is affected by alkyl group electron donation

Checkpoint 5: Acid Strength

Why are alcohols weaker acids than water?

Alkyl groups donate electron density to oxygen, stabilizing the O–H bond and making proton loss less favorable.

Key Terms to Remember

  • Alcohol: Organic compound containing an –OH group
  • Hydroxyl group: –OH functional group
  • Hydrogen bonding: Strong intermolecular attraction involving O–H
  • Polarity: Unequal electron distribution in a bond
  • Miscible: Completely soluble in another liquid
  • Weak acid: Partially donates protons in solution
