Cis - Trans and E-Z Isomerism in Alkenes

Some alkenes can exist in more than one spatial arrangement even though their molecular formula and connectivity are the same. This type of isomerism is called geometric (or stereoisomerism).

1. Why Cis-Trans Isomerism Exists

Rotation around a carbon–carbon double bond is restricted because of the π bond. This means groups attached to the double-bonded carbons can be locked in different positions.

  • Single bonds rotate freely
  • Double bonds do not rotate freely
  • This leads to different spatial arrangements

Activity 1: Concept Check

Why don’t alkenes rotate around the C=C bond like alkanes?

Because breaking the π bond would require significant energy. The π bond locks the atoms in place, preventing free rotation.

2. Cis-Trans Isomerism

Cis–trans isomerism applies when:

  • Each carbon of the C=C bond has two different groups
  • There is at least one identical group on both carbons

Cis: identical groups on the same side
Trans: identical groups on opposite sides

Activity 2: Identify Cis or Trans

In 2-butene, where both carbons have a CH₃ group, what determines cis or trans?


If the two CH₃ groups are on the same side → cis-2-butene If they are on opposite sides → trans-2-butene

3. Limitations of Cis–Trans Naming

Cis–trans works only for simple alkenes. When all four groups attached to the double bond are different, we use the E–Z system.


Activity 3: When Cis–Trans Fails

Can cis–trans naming be used if all four substituents are different?

No. When there are four different groups, cis–trans becomes ambiguous. The E–Z system must be used instead.

4. The E-Z System (CIP Priority Rules)

The E–Z system is based on priority, not identical groups. Priorities are assigned using atomic number:

  • Higher atomic number → higher priority
  • If first atoms are the same, move outward until a difference appears

Z (zusammen) = higher-priority groups on the same side
E (entgegen) = higher-priority groups on opposite sides

Activity 4: Priority Check

Which has higher priority: –CH₃ or –Cl?

Chlorine (Cl) has higher priority because it has a higher atomic number than carbon.

5. Comparing Cis–Trans and E–Z

  • Cis–trans: simple, identical groups required
  • E–Z: universal, works for all alkenes
  • Both describe geometry, not connectivity

Activity 5: Choose the Correct System

Which naming system should be used for a fully substituted alkene?

The E–Z system, because cis–trans cannot handle four different substituents.

Key Terms

  • Geometric isomerism: Same formula and bonds, different spatial arrangement
  • Cis: Identical groups on the same side
  • Trans: Identical groups on opposite sides
  • E–Z system: Priority-based stereochemical naming
  • CIP rules: Rules for assigning substituent priority
  • π bond: Side-on overlap preventing free rotation
