Chapter 7 Summary: Aromatic Compounds and Phenols

In this chapter, you learned how aromatic compounds differ from other hydrocarbons, how benzene is structured and named, and how phenols behave as a special class of aromatic compounds.

1. Aromatic Compounds

  • Aromatic compounds contain one or more benzene rings.
  • Benzene (C₆H₆) is unusually stable due to delocalized π electrons.
  • Aromaticity requires a planar ring, conjugated π system, and delocalized electrons.

Checkpoint: Aromatic vs Non-Aromatic

Why is benzene more stable than cyclohexatriene?

Benzene has delocalized π electrons spread evenly around the ring, lowering its overall energy.

2. Naming Aromatic Compounds

  • The benzene ring often acts as the parent structure.
  • Substituents are named and numbered to give the lowest possible locants.
  • Disubstituted benzenes may use ortho- (1,2), meta- (1,3), and para- (1,4).
  • For IUPAC names, numerical positions are always acceptable.

Checkpoint: Naming Logic

Which name is more systematic: para-chlorotoluene or 4-chlorotoluene?

4-chlorotoluene is more systematic because it uses numerical locants.

3. Phenols

  • A phenol has an –OH group directly attached to a benzene ring.
  • Phenol is the parent name when –OH is present.
  • The –OH group determines numbering and has naming priority.
  • Phenols are more acidic than alcohols due to resonance stabilization.

Checkpoint: Functional Group Priority

Why is a compound named chlorophenol rather than hydroxychlorobenzene?

Because –OH is the principal functional group and defines the parent name as phenol.

4. Common Naming Patterns You Should Recognize

  • Ortho (2-), Meta (3-), Para (4-) describe relative positions.
  • Substituents are listed alphabetically.
  • Numbering always starts at the highest-priority group.

Checkpoint: Position Identification

If a methyl group is at carbon 3 relative to –OH, which term describes it?

Meta — carbon 3 relative to the hydroxyl group.

5. Key Takeaways

  • Aromatic stability comes from electron delocalization.
  • Benzene naming follows strict numbering and substitution rules.
  • Phenols are a distinct functional group with unique acidity.
  • Correct naming depends on identifying the parent structure first.
