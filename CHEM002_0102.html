Lesson 2: Organic vs Inorganic Compounds

Organic and inorganic compounds differ mainly in their composition, bonding, and physical properties. Organic compounds are built around carbon atoms—often bonded to hydrogen—while inorganic compounds typically do not include carbon–hydrogen bonds and often involve metals or ionic structures.

1. Key Differences Between Organic and Inorganic Compounds

Property Organic Compounds Inorganic Compounds
Main Elements C, H, O, N, S Metals, nonmetals, ionic compounds
Bond Type Covalent bonds Often ionic bonds
Physical State Gases, liquids, low-melting solids High-melting solids
Flammability Often flammable Usually nonflammable
Solubility Nonpolar solvents Water & polar solvents
Electrical Conductivity Poor conductors Good conductors (salts in solution)
Complexity Long, complex structures Simpler formulas

Methane (CH₄) – a simple organic molecule.

Sodium chloride (NaCl) – a common inorganic ionic compound.

4. Summary

  • Organic compounds contain carbon–hydrogen bonds; inorganic compounds generally do not.
  • Organic molecules often have covalent bonds and complex structures.
  • Inorganic compounds are often ionic and have higher melting points.
  • Examples: Organic – methane, glucose; Inorganic – NaCl, H₂O.

Key Terms

TermDefinition
Organic compoundCarbon-based compound with C–H bonds.
Inorganic compoundCompound typically lacking C–H bonds.
Covalent bondBond formed by sharing electrons between atoms.
Ionic bondBond formed by transfer of electrons.
SolubilityAbility of a substance to dissolve in a solvent.
