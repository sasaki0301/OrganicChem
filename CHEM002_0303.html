Resonance Structures

Resonance describes molecules for which a single Lewis structure cannot accurately represent the electron distribution. The real structure is a resonance hybrid — a blend of all valid resonance contributors.

1. What is Resonance?

When multiple valid electron arrangements are possible for the same arrangement of atoms, each arrangement is a resonance structure. These structures differ only in electron placement — never atom positions.

Nitrite (NO₂⁻): the double bond can be placed on either oxygen.

Activity 1: Concept Check

Does resonance mean the molecule switches back and forth between forms?

No. The molecule does not flip between structures. The real structure is a hybrid with delocalized electrons.

2. When Do We Use Resonance?

Resonance applies when:

  • A double bond can be placed in more than one position
  • A lone pair can form a double bond
  • A negative or positive charge can be placed on different atoms
  • π bonds can shift positions without breaking σ bonds

Only electrons move. The atomic skeleton must remain unchanged.

Activity 2: Valid or Invalid?

Which of the following actions is NOT allowed in resonance structures?

  • A) Moving a π bond to another position
  • B) Converting a lone pair into a π bond
  • C) Moving atoms to a new location
C is not allowed. Resonance never moves atoms.

3. Resonance Patterns You Must Know

Pattern 1: Lone Pair → Double Bond

Pattern 2: π Bond → Lone Pair

Pattern 3: π Bond → π Bond

Activity 3: Identify the Allowed Move

Which type of electron pair can move in resonance?

  • A) σ bond electrons
  • B) π bond electrons
  • C) Lone pairs
B and C can move. σ bonds must stay intact.

4. Major vs Minor Contributors

Major contributor characteristics:

  • Full octets on all atoms
  • Negative charge on electronegative atoms
  • Positive charge on less electronegative atoms
  • Minimal charge separation

Activity 4: Major Contributor

Which contributor is more stable?

  1. A: Negative charge on oxygen
  2. B: Negative charge on carbon
A is more stable because oxygen stabilizes negative charge better than carbon.

5. Resonance in Ozone (O₃)

Both O–O bonds are identical due to delocalized electrons.

Activity 5: Ozone Hybrid

Why do both O–O bonds have the same length?

Because π electrons are delocalized over both O–O bonds, giving each bond partial double-bond character.

6. Resonance in Carboxylate Ions (RCOO⁻)

Carboxylate ions stabilize negative charge by delocalizing it over two oxygen atoms.

Both C–O bonds are equal; the charge is shared.

Activity 6: Carboxylate Stability

Why is the carboxylate ion more stable than an alcohol’s O⁻?

Because the negative charge is delocalized over two oxygens instead of concentrated on one atom.

7. Aromatic Resonance: Benzene and Phenoxide

Benzene’s π electrons are fully delocalized across all six carbons, creating equal bond lengths.

Phenoxide ion (deprotonated phenol) also delocalizes negative charge into the aromatic ring.

Activity 7: Aromatic Stability

Why is benzene unusually stable?

Because its π electrons are fully delocalized and spread evenly over the ring, creating aromatic stabilization.

8. Allylic Resonance

Allylic systems have a π bond adjacent to a carbon with a lone pair or charge. Electrons can flow across three atoms.

Activity 8: Allylic Check

Which is more stable: a typical carbocation or an allylic carbocation?

The allylic carbocation because the positive charge is delocalized across two carbons.

Key Terms

  • Resonance: Multiple Lewis structures describing one molecule.
  • Hybrid: The true structure formed from all contributors.
  • Major contributor: Most stable resonance form.
  • Delocalization: Electrons shared across multiple atoms.
  • Allylic: Position next to a π bond.
  • Aromatic: Cyclic, conjugated, highly stabilized π system.
  • Carboxylate: RCOO⁻ ion with equal C–O bonds.
