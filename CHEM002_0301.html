Polar Covalent Bonds and Electronegativity

In this lesson, you will learn why some covalent bonds are evenly shared (nonpolar) while others are unevenly shared (polar). The key idea behind this is a property called electronegativity.

1. What is Electronegativity?

Electronegativity is the ability of an atom to attract shared electrons in a chemical bond. The higher the electronegativity, the more strongly an atom pulls electrons toward itself.

General trends in the periodic table:

  • Electronegativity increases from left to right across a period.
  • It decreases from top to bottom in a group.
  • Fluorine (F) is the most electronegative element.

Electronegativity increases toward the upper-right corner of the periodic table.

Activity 1: Predicting Trends

Which atom is more electronegative—oxygen (O) or sulfur (S)? Explain based on periodic trends.

Oxygen is more electronegative than sulfur.
Reason: Oxygen is higher in the same group, and electronegativity decreases down a group.

2. Covalent Bonds: Equal vs Unequal Sharing

A covalent bond forms when two atoms share electrons. However, the sharing may not always be equal. This depends on how big the difference in electronegativity is.

Types of covalent bonds based on electronegativity difference:

  • Nonpolar covalent: nearly equal sharing, EN difference ≈ 0–0.4 (e.g., H–H, C–H)
  • Polar covalent: unequal sharing, EN difference ≈ 0.5–1.8 (e.g., H–Cl, O–H)

Greater electronegativity difference produces a more polar bond.

Activity 2: Classifying Bonds

Classify each bond as nonpolar covalent or polar covalent: A) H–H B) H–Cl C) C–H

A) H–H → Nonpolar covalent (same atom)
B) H–Cl → Polar covalent (large EN difference)
C) C–H → Slightly polar but treated as nonpolar in organic chemistry

3. How Polar Covalent Bonds Create Partial Charges

When electrons are pulled more strongly by one atom, the bond develops partial charges:

  • The atom that pulls electrons becomes δ– (partial negative).
  • The atom that loses electron density becomes δ+ (partial positive).

The arrow indicates direction of electron pull (toward δ–).

Activity 3: Direction of Polarity

Draw the dipole arrow for the O–H bond (in water). Which atom is δ+ and which is δ–?

Oxygen is δ– (more electronegative). Hydrogen is δ+.
Dipole arrow: H → O

4. Bond Polarity vs Molecular Polarity

A molecule can have polar bonds but still be nonpolar overall if the shape is symmetrical (vectors cancel out).

  • CO₂ → linear → dipoles cancel → nonpolar molecule.
  • H₂O → bent → dipoles add → polar molecule.

Molecular geometry determines whether dipoles cancel or reinforce.

Activity 4: Shape and Polarity

Predict whether NH₃ (ammonia) is polar or nonpolar. Explain using shape and dipoles.

NH₃ is polar.
Reason: It has a trigonal pyramidal shape due to a lone pair on nitrogen. The dipoles do not cancel → net dipole pointing toward nitrogen.

5. Application: Predicting Polarity in Organic Molecules

Organic molecules often contain polar bonds (e.g., O–H, C–O, C–Cl) that affect:

  • solubility (polar molecules dissolve in water)
  • boiling point
  • reactivity

Activity 5: Polarity Analysis

Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) dissolves well in water, but hexane (C₆H₁₄) does not. Use polarity to explain why.

Ethanol contains an O–H bond, which is highly polar → allows hydrogen bonding with water. Hexane contains only nonpolar C–H and C–C bonds → cannot interact strongly with water. Therefore: ethanol dissolves in water; hexane does not.
