Chemical Bonding Theory — Step by Step

This lesson explains how atoms form bonds, why different bond types exist, and how bonding influences molecular properties. Work through the short activities to check understanding — use the Show Answer toggle only after you attempt each question.

1. Why atoms bond

Atoms bond to reach a more stable (lower energy) arrangement. Typically this means achieving a filled valence shell — for many atoms this is the noble gas configuration (octet rule for many second-row elements).

Activity 1: Stability and the Octet Rule

Explain, in your own words, why a sodium atom tends to lose an electron while a chlorine atom tends to gain one.

Answer: Sodium (Na) has one valence electron in a 3s orbital; losing it gives the Na⁺ ion a full octet in the previous shell (like neon). Chlorine (Cl) has seven valence electrons; gaining one gives Cl⁻ a full octet. This change lowers the total energy, making the ions more stable.

2. Ionic bonding

Definition: Ionic bonding occurs when one atom transfers electrons to another, producing oppositely charged ions that attract each other.

Typical features: metals + nonmetals, formation of cations and anions, high melting points, crystalline solids, conducts electricity when molten or dissolved.

Example: NaCl — Na transfers an electron to Cl to form Na⁺ and Cl⁻ which pack into an ionic lattice.

Activity 2: Identify Ionic vs Covalent

Which of the following pairs would most likely form an ionic bond? A) Na + Cl B) C + O C) H + H

Answer: A) Na + Cl. Sodium (a metal) and chlorine (a nonmetal) differ greatly in electronegativity; sodium tends to lose an electron while chlorine gains — forming ions and an ionic bond.

3. Covalent bonding

Definition: Covalent bonds form when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. This commonly occurs between nonmetal atoms.

We distinguish nonpolar covalent (equal sharing) from polar covalent (unequal sharing) based on electronegativity differences.

Line formulas (single, double bonds) represent shared electron pairs between atoms.

Activity 3: Predict Bond Type

Predict the bond type between the following atom pairs (Ionic / Polar covalent / Nonpolar covalent): A) H–H B) H–Cl C) Na–Cl

Answer: A) H–H = Nonpolar covalent (same atom, equal sharing). B) H–Cl = Polar covalent (Cl more electronegative than H, electrons pulled toward Cl). C) Na–Cl = Ionic (large electronegativity difference, electron transfer).

Activity 4: Simple Lewis Structure

Draw the Lewis structure for H2O (water). Then explain how the bonding leads to a bent molecular shape.

Answer: Lewis structure: O in center with two lone pairs and two H atoms bonded by single bonds. Electron pairs (bonding & lone pairs) repel; lone pairs exert slightly more repulsion, producing a bent shape (~104.5°). VSEPR explains the geometry.

4. Metallic bonding & coordinate (dative) bonds

Metallic bonding: Atoms in metals share a 'sea of electrons' that are delocalized across the lattice — explains conductivity and malleability.

Coordinate (dative) bond: A covalent bond where one atom donates both electrons in the shared pair (common in Lewis acid-base reactions).

Activity 5: Recognize a Coordinate Bond

Which of the following shows a coordinate bond? A) NH3 → H+ forming NH4+ B) H2 + Cl2 C) NaCl formation

Answer: A) NH3 donating its lone pair to a proton (H⁺) forms NH4+ via a coordinate bond (both electrons of the bond come from N).

5. Bond polarity, bond length & bond energy

Bond polarity: arises from differences in electronegativity — creates partial charges (δ⁺ / δ⁻) within the bond.

Bond length: average distance between nuclei of two bonded atoms (shorter bonds usually stronger).

Bond energy: energy required to break a bond — multiple bonds (double, triple) are stronger and shorter than single bonds.

Activity 6: Compare Bond Lengths & Energies

Which has the shortest bond length and highest bond energy: C–C single bond, C=C double bond, or C≡C triple bond?

Answer: C≡C (triple bond) has the shortest bond length and highest bond energy; C=C is intermediate; C–C single is the longest and weakest of the three.
