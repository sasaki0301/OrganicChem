Chapter 4 Summary: Alkanes and Cycloalkanes

This chapter focused on the structure, naming, and isomerism of saturated hydrocarbons. Mastery of these concepts is essential before moving on to unsaturated hydrocarbons and functionalized compounds.

1. Alkanes and Alkane Isomers

  • Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons containing only single (σ) bonds.
  • General formula: CnH2n+2.
  • Isomers have the same molecular formula but different connectivity.

Checkpoint 1

How many structural isomers does C₅H₁₂ have?

Three: n-pentane, isopentane (2-methylbutane), and neopentane (2,2-dimethylpropane).

2. Alkyl Groups

  • Alkyl groups are formed when one hydrogen is removed from an alkane.
  • They act as substituents in branched hydrocarbons.
  • Common examples: methyl (–CH₃), ethyl (–CH₂CH₃), propyl.

Checkpoint 2

What alkyl group is derived from propane?

Propyl (–CH₂CH₂CH₃) or isopropyl (–CH(CH₃)₂).

3. Naming Alkanes (IUPAC)

  • Identify the longest continuous carbon chain.
  • Number the chain to give the lowest locants.
  • Name substituents and list them alphabetically.
  • Use prefixes (di-, tri-) for repeated substituents.

Checkpoint 3

Name the compound: CH₃–CH(CH₃)–CH₂–CH₃

2-methylbutane.

4. Cycloalkanes

  • Cycloalkanes are alkanes arranged in a ring.
  • General formula: CnH2n.
  • Ring strain affects stability (cyclopropane < cyclobutane < cyclohexane).

Checkpoint 4

Why is cyclohexane more stable than cyclopropane?

Cyclohexane minimizes angle strain and torsional strain by adopting a chair conformation.

5. Naming Cycloalkanes

  • The ring is the parent unless a substituent has more carbons.
  • Number the ring to give lowest possible locants.
  • Alphabetize substituents.

Checkpoint 5

Name a cyclohexane ring with a single ethyl substituent.

Ethylcyclohexane (no number needed for a single substituent).

6. Cis–Trans Isomerism in Cycloalkanes

  • Occurs when two substituents are attached to a ring.
  • Cis: substituents on the same side (up/up or down/down).
  • Trans: substituents on opposite sides (up/down).
  • Up/down orientation defines cis/trans — not axial/equatorial.

Checkpoint 6

Can 1-methylcyclopentane show cis–trans isomerism?

No. Cis–trans requires at least two substituents on the ring.

Key Takeaways

  • Structure determines naming and properties.
  • Branching creates structural isomers.
  • Rings restrict rotation, enabling stereoisomerism.
  • Accurate naming relies on systematic IUPAC rules.
