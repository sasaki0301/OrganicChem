Hybridization, Sigma and Pi Bonds

This lesson explains why atoms form certain shapes, how orbitals mix (hybridize), and how σ (sigma) and π (pi) bonds form from orbital overlap. Work through the activities to strengthen your understanding.

1. What is Hybridization?

Hybridization is the process in which atomic orbitals blend to form new, identical hybrid orbitals. This explains the shapes and bond angles of molecules that cannot be predicted using only pure s and p orbitals.

For example, carbon’s 2s and 2p orbitals mix to form four sp³ hybrid orbitals arranged in a tetrahedral shape. This explains methane’s (CH₄) 109.5° bond angle.

sp³ hybrid orbitals produce the tetrahedral shape of methane.

Activity 1 — Why Carbon Forms Four Bonds

Carbon is 1s² 2s² 2p². How can it form four identical single bonds in CH₄?

Carbon promotes one 2s electron into the empty 2p orbital → 4 unpaired electrons. Then 2s + 3p orbitals hybridize → 4 equivalent sp³ orbitals. Each overlaps with H 1s orbitals → 4 identical σ bonds.

2. Types of Hybridization

Hybridization depends on the number of electron regions (bonds + lone pairs) around an atom:

  • sp — 2 regions → linear (180°)
  • sp² — 3 regions → trigonal planar (120°)
  • sp³ — 4 regions → tetrahedral (109.5°)

Hybrid orbitals differ in shape, orientation, and bond angles.

Determine the hybridization of the central atom in: CO₂, BF₃, CH₄.

3. Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds

Chemical bonds form when orbitals overlap:

  • Sigma (σ) bond — first/primary bond; formed by head-on overlap.
  • Pi (π) bond — formed by side-by-side overlap of unhybridized p orbitals.

Double bonds = 1 σ + 1 π. Triple bonds = 1 σ + 2 π.

Key Terms

  • Hybridization — mixing of atomic orbitals to form hybrid orbitals.
  • sp, sp², sp³ — types of hybrid orbitals.
  • Sigma (σ) bond — strong, head-on orbital overlap.
  • Pi (π) bond — side-by-side overlap of unhybridized p orbitals.
  • Tetrahedral — 109.5° bond angle; typical of sp³ hybridization.
  • Trigonal planar — 120° bond angle; sp² hybridization.
  • Linear — 180° bond angle; sp hybridization.
