Naming Alkenes

Alkenes are hydrocarbons that contain at least one carbon–carbon double bond (C=C). In this lesson, you will learn how to name alkenes systematically using IUPAC rules.

1. What Makes an Alkene?

  • Contains at least one C=C double bond
  • General formula: CnH2n
  • Uses the suffix -ene

Ethene: the simplest alkene

2. Step 1 – Find the Longest Chain with the Double Bond

The parent chain must:

  • Contain the C=C double bond
  • Be the longest possible chain that includes it

Activity 1

Which chain should be chosen as the parent?

The chain that includes the double bond — even if it is not the absolute longest chain.

3. Step 2 – Number the Chain

Number the carbon chain from the end that gives the double bond the lowest possible number.

Activity 2

Why is but-2-ene NOT called but-3-ene?

The double bond must receive the lowest possible number according to IUPAC rules.

4. Step 3 – Name and Position the Double Bond

The position of the double bond is shown by placing the number before the suffix -ene.

  • 2-butene → but-2-ene
  • 1-pentene → pent-1-ene

Activity 3

What does the “2” in hex-2-ene mean?

The double bond starts between carbon 2 and carbon 3.

5. Step 4 – Name Substituents (Branched Alkenes)

If alkyl groups are attached:

  • Name them as prefixes (methyl-, ethyl-, etc.)
  • Number the chain to give the double bond priority
  • List substituents alphabetically

Activity 4

Name the following: a 4-carbon chain with a double bond at C-1 and a methyl at C-3.


3-methylbut-1-ene

6. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Ignoring the double bond when choosing the parent chain
  • ❌ Numbering to give substituents priority over the double bond
  • ❌ Writing “2-butene” instead of but-2-ene
