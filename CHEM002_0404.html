Cycloalkanes

Cycloalkanes are alkanes whose carbon atoms are connected in a ring. They follow the same rules as straight-chain alkanes but have unique shapes, strain, and naming rules.

1. What Are Cycloalkanes?

A cycloalkane is a saturated hydrocarbon where carbon atoms form a closed ring.

  • Contain only single bonds (C–C)
  • General formula: CnH2n
  • Named by adding the prefix “cyclo-” to the alkane name

Examples: cyclopropane (C₃H₆), cyclobutane (C₄H₈), cyclohexane (C₆H₁₂)

2. Shapes and Ring Strain

Rings cannot freely rotate like open-chain alkanes, causing ring strain. Smaller rings are more strained and less stable.

  • Cyclopropane: highly strained (60° angles)
  • Cyclobutane: strained (90° angles)
  • Cyclohexane: low strain, most stable

Activity 2: Stability Check

Which ring is most stable: cyclopropane, cyclobutane, or cyclohexane?

Cyclohexane — It can adopt conformations that minimize strain.

3. Naming Cycloalkanes

Cycloalkanes are named like alkanes but with “cyclo-” in front.

Simple naming:

  • C₃ ring → cyclopropane
  • C₄ ring → cyclobutane
  • C₆ ring → cyclohexane

4. Cycloalkanes vs Alkanes

Even though both are saturated hydrocarbons, cycloalkanes have:

  • Fewer hydrogens (CₙH₂ₙ vs CₙH₂ₙ₊₂)
  • Limited rotation
  • Ring strain
  • Different physical and chemical behavior

5. Substituted Cycloalkanes

When substituents are present:

  • Start numbering at the substituent
  • Number around the ring to give lowest locants
  • Alphabetical order matters

Example: methylcyclohexane

Key Terms

  • Cycloalkane: A ring-shaped saturated hydrocarbon.
  • Ring strain: Instability caused by bond angle distortion.
  • Substituent: Group attached to the main ring.
  • Saturated: Contains only single bonds.
