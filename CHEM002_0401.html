Alkanes & Alkane Isomers

Alkanes are the simplest family of organic compounds — saturated hydrocarbons containing only single C–C and C–H bonds. This lesson explains definition, formulas, typical representations (condensed, structural, skeletal), and how to generate & recognize constitutional (structural) isomers.

1. What are Alkanes?

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons where every carbon is sp³-hybridized and all C–C bonds are single (σ) bonds. They follow the general formula CnH2n+2 for acyclic (open-chain) alkanes.

Common quick facts

  • Also called paraffins.
  • Low reactivity compared to alkenes/alkynes — main reactions: combustion and substitution (radical) reactions.
  • As chain length increases: boiling point increases, volatility decreases.

Common representations

  • Condensed: CH₃CH₂CH₃
  • Structural (Lewis-like): show each bond explicitly
  • Skeletal (line-angle): carbon atoms implied at line ends/intersections — the fastest for organic chemists
Methane — formula: CH₄

2. Constitutional (structural) isomers

Structural or constitutional isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different connectivity of atoms. Example: For C₄H₁₀, there are two isomers: n-butane (straight chain) and isobutane (branched).

Why important?

Isomers often have different physical and chemical properties (boiling point, melting point, reactivity). Recognizing possible isomers is a key organic skill.

How to systematically find isomers

  1. Count carbons (n) from the formula.
  2. Start with the straight chain (n carbons in a row).
  3. Create unique branchings without duplicating connectivity already counted (consider symmetry).
  4. Use line-angle (skeletal) formula to check duplicates — rotate and reflect to test identity.
n-Butane and Isobutane (C₄H₁₀)

3. Worked examples — generate isomers

Activity A: C₄H₁₀ (Butanes)

List the constitutional isomers of C₄H₁₀ and provide their condensed names.

Answer:
1) n-butane — condensed: CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃
2) isobutane (or methylpropane) — condensed: (CH₃)₃CH
These two are the only constitutional isomers for C₄H₁₀.

Activity B: C₅H₁₂ (Pentanes)

How many constitutional isomers exist for C₅H₁₂? Name them and show skeletal sketches (describe if sketching on paper).

Answer:
There are three isomers:
  1. n-pentane — CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂CH₃
  2. isopentane (methylbutane) — CH₃CH(CH₃)CH₂CH₃
  3. neopentane (dimethylpropane) — C(CH₃)₄ (condensed: (CH₃)₄C)
Sketch tip: draw straight chain, then move methyl groups to create unique branching; avoid duplicates from rotation/reflection.

Activity C: C₆H₁₄ (Hexanes)

How many constitutional isomers does C₆H₁₄ have? List them (common names acceptable).

Answer:
C₆H₁₄ has five constitutional isomers: n-hexane, 2-methylpentane (isohexane), 3-methylpentane, 2,2-dimethylbutane, and 2,3-dimethylbutane. (Practice: sketch each and verify distinct connectivity.)

5. Further Practice

Activity D: Recognize duplicates

Sketch two different-looking skeletal drawings. How do you test if they are the same isomer or different?

Answer:
Rotate and reflect the sketches. If after rotation/reflection the connectivity matches (same carbon-to-carbon connections and branch positions), they are the same. If not, they are different isomers. A systematic way: label carbons and compare the adjacency list.

Activity E: Boiling point trend

Which has higher boiling point: n-hexane or 2,2-dimethylbutane? Explain.

Answer:
n-Hexane has a higher boiling point. Straighter chains pack better and have larger surface area → stronger London dispersion forces. Highly branched isomers are more compact and have lower boiling points.

Key Terms

TermDefinition
AlkaneSaturated hydrocarbon with only single bonds (general formula CnH2n+2 for open chains).
Condensed formulaShorthand showing groups (e.g., CH3CH2CH3).
Skeletal (line-angle) formulaCarbon atoms at line ends/intersections; hydrogens on carbons usually omitted.
Constitutional (structural) isomerSame molecular formula, different connectivity of atoms.
Branched isomerAn isomer where one or more carbon atoms branch off the main chain.
