Chapter 3 Summary – Polarity, Charges, Resonance, Acids, and Bases

This chapter explored how electrons move, distribute, and influence molecular behavior. Understanding these concepts allows you to predict reactivity, stability, acidity/basicity, and physical properties of organic molecules.

1. Polar Covalent Bonds & Electronegativity

  • Electronegativity measures an atom’s ability to attract electrons.
  • A bond is nonpolar when atoms share electrons equally.
  • A bond is polar when one atom attracts electrons more strongly (dipole).
  • Molecular polarity depends on both bond polarity and molecular geometry.

Checkpoint: Polarity Test

Why is CO₂ nonpolar even though the C=O bonds are polar?

The dipoles cancel because the molecule is linear and symmetrical → net dipole = 0.

2. Formal Charge

  • Formal charge = valence electrons − (nonbonding + bonding/2).
  • Used to determine the best (most stable) Lewis structure.
  • Best structures usually have:
    • Lowest overall formal charges
    • Negative charges on electronegative atoms
    • Full octets

Checkpoint: Quick Calculation

What is the formal charge of nitrogen in NH₄⁺?

Formal Charge = 5 − 0 − (8/2) = +1

3. Resonance

  • Resonance occurs when electrons can be placed in more than one valid arrangement.
  • Electrons (not atoms) move: lone pairs & π bonds shift.
  • The actual molecule is a resonance hybrid.
  • Major contributors have:
    • Full octets
    • Lowest charges
    • Negative charge on electronegative atoms
    • Minimal separation of charges

Checkpoint: Resonance Validity

What rule makes this invalid? “Moving atoms to a new position.”

Resonance moves only electrons, never atoms.

4. Bronsted–Lowry Acids and Bases

  • Acid = proton donor (H⁺).
  • Base = proton acceptor.
  • Conjugate base = what remains after an acid donates H⁺.
  • Strength increases with stability of conjugate base.

Checkpoint: Conjugate Pairs

What is the conjugate base of HSO₄⁻?

Remove H⁺ → SO₄²⁻

5. Lewis Acids and Bases

  • Lewis acid = electron pair acceptor.
  • Lewis base = electron pair donor.
  • All Bronsted bases are Lewis bases, but not all Bronsted acids are Lewis acids.

Checkpoint: Identify Type

Is BF₃ a Lewis acid or base?

Lewis acid — lacks an electron pair and accepts one.

6. Organic Acids and Bases

  • Acid strength depends on stability of conjugate base.
  • Carboxylic acids are strong because their conjugate base has two resonance structures.
  • Electron-withdrawing groups increase acidity (inductive effect).
  • Bases:
    • Aliphatic amines = strong bases
    • Aromatic amines = weaker (lone pair delocalized)
    • Carbanions = extremely strong bases

Checkpoint: Acid Ranking

Rank increasing acidity: ethanol, phenol, acetic acid.

Ethanol < Phenol < Acetic acid (due to resonance stabilization in phenoxide and carboxylate)
