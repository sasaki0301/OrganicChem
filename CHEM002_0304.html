Acids and Bases – Arrhenius, Brønsted–Lowry, and Lewis

In this lesson, you’ll review the three major definitions of acids and bases, learn how to recognize conjugate pairs, and practice identifying strong vs. weak acids and bases. This general chemistry foundation will prepare you for the next lesson on organic acids and bases.

1. Arrhenius Acids and Bases

The Arrhenius definition is the simplest way to think about acids and bases in aqueous (water) solutions:

  • Arrhenius acid: produces H⁺ (or H₃O⁺) ions in water.
  • Arrhenius base: produces OH⁻ ions in water.

Examples:

  • HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
  • NaOH(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

This definition works well for many strong acids and bases in water but is limited because it only applies to aqueous solutions and cannot describe all acid–base reactions.

Activity 1: Arrhenius Check

Which of the following substances is an Arrhenius base in water?

  • A) HNO₃
  • B) NaOH
  • C) HCl
  • D) CO₂
Answer: B) NaOH
NaOH dissociates in water to release OH⁻, so it is an Arrhenius base.

2. Brønsted–Lowry Acids and Bases

The Brønsted–Lowry definition is more general and is very important in chemistry:

  • Brønsted–Lowry acid: proton (H⁺) donor.
  • Brønsted–Lowry base: proton (H⁺) acceptor.

This definition does not require water. Any reaction where one species donates H⁺ and another accepts it is an acid–base reaction.

Conjugate Acid–Base Pairs

In every Brønsted–Lowry reaction, acids and bases form conjugate pairs:

  • The acid becomes its conjugate base after losing H⁺.
  • The base becomes its conjugate acid after gaining H⁺.

Example: HCl + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻

  • HCl = acid → conjugate base = Cl⁻
  • H₂O = base → conjugate acid = H₃O⁺

Activity 2: Identify Conjugate Pairs

In the reaction: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻

Identify the acid, base, conjugate acid, and conjugate base.

  • Base: NH₃ (accepts H⁺)
  • Conjugate acid: NH₄⁺ (NH₃ after gaining H⁺)
  • Acid: H₂O (donates H⁺)
  • Conjugate base: OH⁻ (H₂O after losing H⁺)

Every acid–base reaction has two conjugate pairs.

3. Lewis Acids and Bases

The Lewis definition focuses on electron pairs rather than protons:

  • Lewis acid: electron pair acceptor.
  • Lewis base: electron pair donor.

Many metal ions and electron-poor molecules act as Lewis acids, while species with lone pairs (like NH₃, H₂O) often act as Lewis bases.

Example: BF₃ + NH₃ → F₃B←NH₃

  • BF₃ is the Lewis acid (accepts a lone pair).
  • NH₃ is the Lewis base (donates its lone pair on N).
  • The arrow indicates that the lone pair of electrons from nitrogen is donated to the vacant orbital of boron to form a single bond.

Activity 3: Brønsted vs Lewis

In the reaction: H⁺ + :OH⁻ → H₂O (The colon “:” represents a lone pair on oxygen.)

Classify H⁺ and OH⁻ as Brønsted and Lewis acid/base.

  • H⁺: Brønsted acid (proton donor), Lewis acid (accepts an electron pair).
  • OH⁻: Brønsted base (proton acceptor), Lewis base (donates an electron pair).

The same species can be described with both models.

4. Strong vs Weak Acids and Bases

Strong acids/bases completely ionize in water. Weak acids/bases only partially ionize.

Examples of Strong Acids (in water)

  • HCl, HBr, HI
  • HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄

Examples of Strong Bases (in water)

  • Group 1 hydroxides: NaOH, KOH
  • Some Group 2 hydroxides: Ca(OH)₂, Ba(OH)₂

Weak acids and bases (like CH₃COOH or NH₃) exist in equilibrium between ionized and unionized forms.

Activity 4: Classify Strength

Classify each as strong acid, weak acid, strong base, or weak base in water:

  • A) HCl
  • B) CH₃COOH
  • C) NaOH
  • D) NH₃
  • HCl → strong acid
  • CH₃COOH → weak acid
  • NaOH → strong base
  • NH₃ → weak base

Strong = almost fully ionized; weak = partially ionized at equilibrium.

5. Amphoteric Substances and Water

Some substances can act as either an acid or a base depending on the reaction. These are called amphoteric (or amphiprotic if Brønsted).

Water (H₂O) is a classic example:

  • With a strong acid (like HCl), H₂O acts as a base: HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻
  • With a strong base (like NH₃), H₂O acts as an acid: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻

Activity 5: Amphoteric Check

In the reaction: HCO₃⁻ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ + OH⁻ Is HCO₃⁻ acting as an acid or a base?

HCO₃⁻ is acting as an acid.
It donates a proton (H⁺) to water to form H₂CO₃, while water becomes OH⁻.

6. pH and pOH (Quick Overview)

The pH scale measures the acidity of a solution based on the concentration of H⁺ (or H₃O⁺).

  • pH < 7 → acidic
  • pH = 7 → neutral (pure water at 25 °C)
  • pH > 7 → basic (alkaline)

In more advanced work, you will use pH, pOH, and pKₐ to compare acid strength. For now, remember: lower pH = more acidic; higher pH = more basic.

Activity 6: Simple pH Comparison

Which solution is more acidic: one with pH 3.0 or one with pH 6.0? Briefly explain.

The solution with pH 3.0 is more acidic.
Each unit decrease in pH represents a tenfold increase in [H⁺], so pH 3 has much more H⁺ than pH 6.

7. Additional Practice Problems

Try these without looking at the answers first. These cover Arrhenius, Brønsted–Lowry, Lewis definitions, conjugate pairs, strength, and amphoterism.

Practice 1: Classify Each Species

For each substance below, classify it as an Arrhenius acid, Arrhenius base, or neither:

  • A) HBr
  • B) Ca(OH)₂
  • C) NH₃
  • D) CH₄
  • A) HBr → Arrhenius acid (forms H⁺ in water)
  • B) Ca(OH)₂ → Arrhenius base (releases OH⁻)
  • C) NH₃ → Neither (Arrhenius), because it does not contain OH⁻ — but it is a Brønsted base.
  • D) CH₄ → Neither (no acid–base behavior in water)

Practice 2: Identify All Acid–Base Roles

For the reaction: HSO₄⁻ + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + SO₄²⁻ Identify:
• Acid
• Base
• Conjugate acid
• Conjugate base

  • HSO₄⁻ → acid (donates H⁺)
  • H₂O → base (accepts H⁺)
  • H₃O⁺ → conjugate acid
  • SO₄²⁻ → conjugate base

HSO₄⁻ is amphiprotic, but here it acts as an acid.

Practice 3: Lewis Acid or Lewis Base?

Determine whether each is a Lewis acid or Lewis base:

  • A) BF₃
  • B) H₂O
  • C) Fe³⁺
  • D) NH₃
  • BF₃ → Lewis acid (electron pair acceptor)
  • H₂O → Lewis base (lone pairs on oxygen)
  • Fe³⁺ → Lewis acid (electron-poor metal ion)
  • NH₃ → Lewis base (donates lone pair on N)

Practice 4: Predict Reaction Direction

Which direction is favored in this Brønsted–Lowry acid–base equilibrium?

HCl + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻

Explain based on acid strength.

The reaction strongly favors the products (→ direction).

HCl is a strong acid, which means it donates its proton completely. Strong acids have weak conjugate bases (Cl⁻), so equilibrium lies almost entirely on the right.

Practice 5: Amphoteric Behavior Analysis

Show whether HCO₃⁻ acts as an acid or a base in each reaction:

A) HCO₃⁻ + OH⁻ → CO₃²⁻ + H₂O B) HCO₃⁻ + H₃O⁺ → H₂CO₃ + H₂O

  • A) HCO₃⁻ acts as an acid (donates H⁺ to OH⁻)
  • B) HCO₃⁻ acts as a base (accepts H⁺ from H₃O⁺)

HCO₃⁻ is amphiprotic: it can donate or accept H⁺ depending on the environment.

Practice 6: pH and Strength Reasoning

A solution has a pH of 2. Another has a pH of 5.

How many times more acidic is the pH 2 solution? Explain.

The pH 2 solution is 1000× more acidic.

Each pH unit = ×10 difference in [H⁺]. Difference = 5 − 2 = 3 → 10³ = 1000×.

Key Terms

  • Arrhenius acid: Produces H⁺ (or H₃O⁺) in water.
  • Arrhenius base: Produces OH⁻ in water.
  • Brønsted–Lowry acid: Proton (H⁺) donor.
  • Brønsted–Lowry base: Proton (H⁺) acceptor.
  • Conjugate acid–base pair: Two species that differ by one H⁺.
  • Lewis acid: Electron pair acceptor.
  • Lewis base: Electron pair donor.
  • Strong acid/base: Almost completely ionized in water.
  • Weak acid/base: Only partially ionized; exists in equilibrium.
  • Amphoteric (amphiprotic): Can act as acid or base (e.g., H₂O, HCO₃⁻).
  • pH: Measure of acidity based on [H⁺].
