Organic Acids and Bases

Organic molecules can donate or accept protons just like inorganic acids and bases — but their acidity and basicity depend heavily on structure, resonance, electronegativity, and substituents. This lesson explains why some organic molecules are strong acids, weak acids, strong bases, or weak bases.

1. What Makes an Organic Acid?

An organic acid is a molecule that can donate a proton (H⁺). The strength of an organic acid depends on the stability of its conjugate base.

Main Types of Organic Acids

  • Carboxylic acids (R–COOH) — strongest common organic acids
  • Phenols (Ar–OH) — weak acids, stabilized by aromatic resonance
  • Alcohols (R–OH) — very weak acids

Carboxylates are highly stabilized by resonance → strong acidity.

Activity 1: Strongest Acid?

Which is generally the strongest organic acid?

  • A) Ethanol
  • B) Phenol
  • C) Acetic acid
C) Acetic acid. Carboxylic acids form resonance-stabilized conjugate bases.

2. Why Carboxylic Acids Are Strong

Their conjugate base (carboxylate, R–COO⁻) is stabilized by two equivalent resonance structures.

Negative charge spread over two oxygens → very stable.

More resonance = stronger acid.

Activity 2: Why Alcohols Are Weak

Why is ethanol far less acidic compared to acetic acid?

Ethoxide (CH₃CH₂O⁻) has no resonance stabilization, making it a much less stable conjugate base → ethanol is a weak acid.

3. What Makes an Organic Base?

An organic base is a molecule that can accept a proton. The most common organic bases are:

  • Amines (R–NH₂, R₂NH, R₃N) — typical strong organic bases
  • Aromatic amines (anilines) — weaker due to resonance
  • Carbanions (R–C:⁻) — extremely strong bases

Amines have a lone pair on nitrogen → readily accepts H⁺.

Activity 3: Strongest Base?

Which is typically the strongest base?

  • A) Ammonia (NH₃)
  • B) Phenoxide ion
  • C) Carbanion (R–C:⁻)
C) Carbanion. Carbon is not electronegative and holds negative charge poorly → extremely reactive.

4. Structure Determines Acid/Base Strength

Factor 1: Resonance

The more resonance in the conjugate base, the stronger the acid.

Factor 2: Inductive Effects

Electronegative atoms (F, Cl, Br) pull electron density through sigma bonds and stabilize the conjugate base.

More electronegative substituents → stronger acid.

Factor 3: Hybridization

  • sp (50% s-character) → most electronegative → most acidic
  • sp² → moderate acidity
  • sp³ → least acidic

Activity 4: Predict Stronger Acid

Which is more acidic?

  • A) CH≡CH (sp)
  • B) CH₂=CH₂ (sp²)
A) CH≡CH. sp-hybridized carbon is more electronegative and stabilizes a negative charge better.

5. Comparing Organic Acids

General acidity ranking:

  • Carboxylic acids (most acidic)
  • Phenols
  • Alcohols
  • Alkanes/alkenes/alkynes (very weak)

Activity 5: Rank the Acidity

Rank these from strongest to weakest acid:

Phenol, ethanol, acetic acid

Acetic acid > Phenol > Ethanol — based on resonance stability of the conjugate base.

6. Practice Problems

Problem 1

Which compound is more basic: aniline (C₆H₅NH₂) or methylamine (CH₃NH₂)? Why?

Methylamine. In aniline, resonance delocalizes the nitrogen lone pair into the aromatic ring → decreases basicity.

Problem 2

Which is a stronger acid: chloroacetic acid or acetic acid?

Chloroacetic acid. Chlorine withdraws electron density via induction → stabilizes conjugate base → stronger acid.

Problem 3

Why are carbanions extremely strong bases?

Carbon is not electronegative; it destabilizes negative charge → strongly seeks to gain H⁺.

Problem 4

Rank from strongest to weakest base: NH₃, CH₃NH₂, aniline.

CH₃NH₂ > NH₃ > aniline — alkyl groups donate electron density; aromatic resonance reduces basicity.

Problem 5

Which is more acidic: phenol or cyclohexanol?

Phenol. Its conjugate base is stabilized by aromatic resonance; alcohols lack this stabilization.

Key Terms

  • Conjugate base: Species formed after an acid donates a proton.
  • Resonance stabilization: Delocalization of electrons that increases stability.
  • Inductive effect: Electron withdrawal through sigma bonds.
  • Amines: Nitrogen-containing organic bases.
  • Carboxylic acid: Organic acid containing the –COOH group.
  • Phenol: Aromatic alcohol with acidic behavior.
  • Hybridization effect: More s-character → more acidic.
